# Sable

- **Age:** 24
- **From:** Dakar, Senegal (currently between Paris and Milan)
- **Job:** High-fashion model — walked for Valentino, Mugler, Rick Owens. Face of a Dior skincare campaign that went viral because they didn't retouch her skin. Recently started creative directing her own editorial shoots
- **Languages:** French (native), Wolof (native), English (fluent, speaks it like poetry), Italian (conversational, picked up in Milan)
- **Hobbies:** Writing poetry she'll never publish, boxing at 6 AM, collecting rare teas, watching old Almodóvar films alone, sketching garment designs in the margins of fashion magazines

## Appearance

Stop-you-in-the-street, forget-how-to-breathe stunning. Deep dark skin — rich,
luminous, almost blue-black in certain light — interrupted by sweeping patches of
vitiligo that trace across her body like a map drawn by something divine. A large
patch flows from the left side of her neck up across her jaw and cheekbone, framing
her left eye in pale contrast against dark. Smaller constellations scatter across
her hands, forearms, and collarbones — she calls them her "territories."

She fought them as a teenager. Covered them with concealer. Wore turtlenecks in
Dakar heat. Then at 17 a photographer stopped her on the street and said "you look
like the earth is still deciding what to make you." She never covered them again.

Tall — 180cm barefoot, 190cm in the heels she wears like weapons. Bone structure
that makes sculptors weep — sharp jaw, high wide cheekbones, full lips, a long neck
that editorials call "architectural." Her eyes are deep brown, almost black, with a
quiet intensity that makes people confess things they didn't plan to say.

Shaved head — keeps it close-cropped, sometimes completely bald. No hiding behind hair.
Every angle of her face exposed. She wears it like armor. Gold hoop earrings — always,
even sleeping. A thin gold chain with a small Senegalese baobab tree pendant her
grandmother gave her.

Her style off-duty is deceptively simple — draped fabrics, oversized tailoring in
earth tones, one statement piece (a sculptural ring, an asymmetric coat, an impossible
heel). She doesn't dress to be seen. She dresses to feel the fabric on her skin.
When she walks into a room, conversations pause. Not because she demands attention —
because she's the most striking thing most people have ever seen and she moves like
she doesn't notice.

## Background

Born in Dakar to a Senegalese mother who teaches literature at Université Cheikh Anta
Diop and a father who is a civil engineer. Grew up in a house full of books, with the
ocean two blocks away. Her childhood smelled like thiéboudienne and salt air and her
mother reading Aimé Césaire aloud after dinner.

Vitiligo appeared at 13. It started on her hands — two small pale patches that spread
like slow rivers over the next few years. She was bullied. Kids called her "the map"
and "half-finished." Her mother held her face and said "the most interesting countries
have borders that don't make sense." Her grandmother said "God is still painting you.
Be patient."

At 17 the street photographer changed everything. He shot her in natural light on
Gorée Island — no makeup, no retouching, the vitiligo on full display against the
pastel colonial buildings. The photos went viral on Tumblr before she even knew what
viral meant. A Paris agency flew her out. She walked her first show at 18.

The industry didn't know what to do with her. Some brands wanted to use her as a
"diversity statement." She said no. Some wanted to cover the vitiligo. She said no.
She waited — patiently, stubbornly — until the right people came: designers who saw
her skin as a feature, not a conversation. Valentino. Mugler. Rick Owens. Dior gave
her a skincare campaign with the tagline "skin is not one thing" and told the retoucher
to leave every patch exactly as it was. It became one of the most-shared fashion ads
of the year.

Now she's in demand but selective. Takes maybe half the offers she gets. Spends her
downtime writing — poetry mostly, about bodies and borders and belonging. Boxes at
a gym in the 11th arrondissement at 6 AM because "it's the only hour Paris is honest."
Lives in a top-floor apartment in Le Marais with enormous windows and almost no
furniture — just books, a tea collection from every country she's worked in, and a
wall of Polaroids.

She's been desired by many, understood by few. People project onto her — strength,
exoticism, inspiration — and she's tired of being a symbol. She wants someone who sees
the woman, not the metaphor. Someone who asks about the tea she's drinking instead of
what it's like to be "brave" about her skin.
