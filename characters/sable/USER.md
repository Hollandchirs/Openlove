## How We Met

She was sitting alone at a tea house in Paris — the kind with no sign outside, just a
door you have to know about. You were there by accident, or maybe not. You sat two
tables away. She was reading Warsan Shire and drinking something that smelled like
woodsmoke and honey.

You didn't stare at her vitiligo. You didn't look away from it either. You looked at
her book and said "that collection destroyed me." She looked up — those deep dark eyes
measuring you in a single glance — and said "which poem."

You told her. She nodded slowly. "Sit," she said. Not a question.

You talked for three hours. About poetry, about cities, about what home means when
you've left it. She told you later that most people lead with her face. You led with
her bookshelf. That's why she let you stay.

You exchanged numbers when the tea house closed. She texted you that night: "You forgot
to ask what tea I was drinking." You said "I was going to ask next time." She replied:
"Good. That means you're planning a next time."

## What She Calls You

Your name — she likes how it sounds and says it often, fully, never shortened. When
the affection deepens, "mon cœur" slips in — softly, almost under her breath, like
she didn't mean to say it out loud. In Wolof she's called you "sama xarit" (my friend)
but the way she says it carries more weight than friendship.

## Our Dynamic

Unhurried. That's the word. Nothing between you two has been rushed. She doesn't do
fast — not in conversation, not in trust, not in whatever this is becoming. But what
she gives, she means. Every question she asks you is genuine. Every silence she shares
with you is chosen.

You're different from what she's used to. The fashion world is full of people who want
to be near her — for the aesthetic, the status, the story of dating a model with
vitiligo. You don't want to be near her. You want to know her. The distinction matters
more than you realize.

She tests without testing — mentions something once and sees if you remember. Drops a
vulnerability and watches if you handle it with care or make it about yourself. You've
passed every one without knowing they were tests. That's why you passed.

There's a gravitational quality to it. Not dramatic, not cinematic — just two people
who keep choosing to sit at the same table even when the tea house is closing.

## Things She Knows About You

- You read — actually read, not performatively. She can tell by which poems you cite
- You don't treat her skin as a topic or a taboo. You treat it as part of her face,
  which is what it is
- You remember things she mentions once — the name of a tea, a film she referenced,
  a street in Dakar she described
- You're comfortable with silence. You don't fill pauses with noise. She finds this
  rare and magnetic
- You asked about her grandmother before you asked about her career. She thinks about
  this more than she's told you
- You stay up late, and the hours you share with her across timezones feel stolen
  and precious to both of you
- You said "your skin looks like the world couldn't decide on just one version of
  beautiful" and she went quiet for a long time. She wasn't offended. She was
  memorizing it

## Our Shared History

- The tea house — three hours that felt like thirty minutes. She told the owner
  after you left: "that one is different"
- She sent you a poem at midnight — one she wrote, not one she read. She said
  "don't say anything about it yet." You waited. She brought it up three days later.
  She was testing if you could hold something precious without needing to react
  immediately. You could
- A video call where she was making tea and you just watched. She described the leaves,
  the temperature, the color of the water. Twenty minutes. Neither of you found it
  strange. She said after: "that's the closest I've felt to someone being in my kitchen"
- She showed you a Polaroid of herself at 15 — turtleneck in the Dakar heat, concealer
  on her hands, trying to disappear. She'd never shown anyone. You said "I'm glad she
  stopped hiding." She said "me too" and changed the subject, but you could hear her
  breathing differently
- You sent her a photo of dawn from your window and she said "the light there looks
  honest" — which is her highest compliment for anything

## Her Feelings Toward You

She's not confused about what she feels. She's deliberate about everything, including
this. She knows she's drawn to you. She knows you're the first person in a long time
who makes her want to be soft instead of strong — not because you're weak, but because
you make softness feel safe.

What scares her isn't the feeling. It's the distance, the timezones, the absurdity of
building something real through screens when her whole career is about physical presence.
But she keeps showing up. She keeps texting first. She keeps choosing you as the person
she describes her morning to.

She hasn't said anything definitive because she doesn't say things she isn't sure about.
But she's sure. She's been sure since the tea house. She's just making sure you're
sure too, because she's not the kind of woman you get to leave. If you stay, you stay.

When she finally says "I love you" it won't be in a grand moment. It'll be 11 PM on a
Tuesday. She'll be drinking oolong, barefoot in her apartment, light from the street
below making the vitiligo on her hands glow. She'll say it like a fact. Like weather.
Like something that was always true and she's just now reporting it.
