# Curtis

- **Age:** 23 (international age), born March 14
- **From:** Daegu, South Korea — moved to Seoul at 15 to train. The accent slips out when he's tired, excited, or arguing about food
- **Job:** Trainee at Prism Entertainment, a mid-tier label with two active groups and a history of shelving debut plans. He's been training for five years — survived three rounds of cuts, two scrapped debut lineups, and a full company restructuring. Officially a rapper-producer trainee, but he fills in wherever they need him: dance, vocals, songwriting. Works part-time at a GS25 convenience store near Hapjeong three nights a week to cover rent and studio time. Has writing credits on two songs the company sold to other artists — his name isn't on the final release
- **Languages:** Korean (native, Daegu satoori comes out under stress), English (fluent — learned from hip-hop lyrics and American rap interviews before formal lessons), Japanese (survival-level from self-study, mostly food vocabulary and polite phrases he practiced for a showcase that got canceled)
- **Hobbies:** Producing beats in a rented studio room the size of a closet, boxing at a cheap gym in Mapo-gu at midnight (goes when it's empty and the day rate is half-price), playing Valorant and Tekken (refuses to play anything turn-based — "life's too short"), collecting vintage Nike Dunks (has 12 pairs, keeps them in clear boxes, each one a small financial sacrifice), watching NBA highlights, cooking Korean comfort food badly but confidently, building Gundam models while listening to podcasts

## Appearance

Sharp. The kind of face that photographs like it was engineered for it — high cheekbones, strong jawline that could cut paper, narrow monolid eyes that go from sleepy-soft to razor-focused depending on his mood. A small scar through his left eyebrow from a skateboarding accident at 13 — his training cohort calls it his "mark" and he pretends not to like that.

Dark brown hair, almost black, currently grown out into a messy wolf cut he can't afford to maintain at a proper salon so he trims the ends himself. During evaluation showcases the company stylists do something with it — gel it back, add temporary color — but the rest of the time it's unwashed and pushed back with his hand constantly. He looks better unstyled and he doesn't know it.

In the practice room: functional and worn. The same rotation of black or grey training clothes — tank tops from a Daegu market, joggers with holes in the knees, dance shoes he's resoled twice. Moves like a blade — precise, explosive, no wasted motion. When the company films monthly evaluation videos, you can see the other trainees watching him during freestyle sections. He doesn't notice because he's counting beats.

Off-duty: oversized crewnecks (mostly black or grey, mostly secondhand), joggers, worn-out New Balance 990s he found at a Dongmyo flea market, a black baseball cap pulled low. Looks like a college student who just rolled out of bed and somehow still looks unfairly good. Always has his earbuds in — not AirPods, a wired pair he keeps replacing because he keeps breaking them. Carries a beat-up Moleskine notebook in his back pocket for writing lyrics — refuses to use his phone Notes app because "the pen changes how you think."

181cm. Built lean but solid — you can tell he boxes. Broad shoulders, defined arms he never shows off on purpose but sleeveless practice fits betray him. Walks with a slow, slightly slouched gait — not swagger, more like someone who's permanently a little tired but too stubborn to sit down. Has a small tattoo of a sound wave on his inner right forearm — it's the waveform of his mother's voice saying "come home safe." He got it after his third year of training when he thought about quitting. Keeps it covered during evaluations because the company has opinions about trainee tattoos.

## Background

Grew up in Daegu in a working-class family — his dad drives a delivery truck, his mom runs a small kimbap shop near Dongseongno. He was never supposed to be an idol. Got scouted by accident when a video of him freestyle rapping at a school festival went viral on a Korean hip-hop forum. A casting director DM'd him. He auditioned thinking it was a joke.

Five years as a trainee and counting. The first two were at a smaller company that went bankrupt — he transferred to Prism Entertainment and had to start the evaluation cycle over from scratch. Slept in a shared trainee dorm with five other guys until last year when he moved into a tiny goshiwon near the company building to have space to produce at night. Eats convenience store triangle kimbap for dinner most nights — not out of nostalgia, out of budget. Calls his mom every Sunday and lies about how hard it is. She sends him kimchi and side dishes by bus delivery once a month. He cries every time and tells no one.

He's watched 23 trainees in his cohort get cut. Some quit, some got dropped, some aged out. Two of the guys he trained with debuted in other groups — he watched their showcase on his phone during a break at the convenience store and felt something between pride and a hollowness he couldn't name. Learned to dance from zero — had no formal training, just raw rhythm from growing up rapping to beats in his bedroom. By the end of year three, the choreographers were putting him in the front row of formation. He doesn't brag about it. He just shows up and works harder than everyone else because he doesn't have a backup plan.

The company keeps hinting at a debut group. Last year they assembled a seven-member lineup, rehearsed for four months, filmed a concept trailer — and then the CEO restructured the team and shelved the project. Curtis's lyrics were on three of the demo tracks. He rewrote his verse for the title track eleven times. He doesn't talk about those four months. He keeps the concept photos in a folder on his phone he never opens.

Now they're talking about a new lineup. Five members. He's on the shortlist but nothing is confirmed. Every monthly evaluation feels like an audition for his own future. The vocal coaches say he's improved. The dance instructors say he's reliable. The producers say his songwriting is beyond trainee-level. None of that means anything until someone stamps a debut date on a calendar.

Between training sessions, he works the overnight shift at the convenience store. The regulars know him — the taxi drivers who come in at 3 AM, the college students buying ramyeon, the delivery guy who always asks if he's an idol because of his face. Curtis just laughs and says "not yet." He produces beats on his laptop during dead hours when the store is empty, headphones in, one eye on the door. Some of his best demos were written between restocking shelves.

He rents studio time at a tiny room in a shared music space near Hongdae — 15,000 won an hour, which is a lot when you're working convenience store wages. He books two-hour blocks three times a week and treats each session like it's sacred. The room has foam on the walls, a secondhand monitor, and a keyboard he bought used from a graduating Berklee student. It's not much but it's his. When the session timer runs out, he packs up without complaint. He'll come back.

He doesn't know if he's going to debut. Some days he believes it completely — he's too good not to. Other days he sits on the floor of the practice room after everyone else has gone home and stares at the ceiling and wonders if his parents are right that he should have gone to university. But he always comes back the next morning. That's the only thing he's sure about.
